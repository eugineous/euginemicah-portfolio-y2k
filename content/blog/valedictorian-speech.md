Standing at that podium at TIBS College, looking out at 400 people — classmates, parents, lecturers — I understood something I'd been circling around for years.

Every story has a room it's meant for.

## The Brief

Valedictorian speech. 10 minutes. Represent the class of 2024. Don't be boring. Don't be generic. Don't cry.

I failed at the last one.

## The Process

I started by asking myself one question: _What do my classmates actually need to hear right now?_

Not what sounds impressive. Not what will make the administration happy. What does a 22-year-old who just finished three years of journalism school, in a country where media jobs are scarce, actually need to hear?

The answer: that the struggle is the credential.

## The Structure

I built the speech in three movements:

**Movement 1 — Acknowledgment.** Name the difficulty. Don't pretend the last three years were easy. They weren't. Acknowledge the sacrifices, the failures, the moments of doubt.

**Movement 2 — Reframe.** Every hardship was training. Every rejection was data. Every late night was compound interest.

**Movement 3 — Challenge.** Don't go out there and get a job. Go out there and build something. The world doesn't need more employees. It needs more builders.

## What It Taught Me

The best speeches — like the best journalism — aren't about the speaker. They're about the audience.

When you make someone feel seen, they'll remember you forever.

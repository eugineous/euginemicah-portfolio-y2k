★ Excerpt ★

From the prologue

A child does not know that he is poor. The child only knows that the lamp goes out at a certain hour, that the rain has opinions about the roof, and that the chicken in the corner of the house has a name.

I was a child in Lugari. The county is called Kakamega now. Before that it had another name. Before that it had the rain. I do not remember anyone being unhappy about any of it. The houses argued with the weather and the weather won most arguments, but the houses kept the families and the families kept each other, and the radio in the corner of the room was on whenever the news came on.

My grandfather had built shops where there had not been shops. The village was named for him. By the time the village called itself Kemoview, the grandfather was a story being told in past tense, but the name kept holding.

We do not get to choose the family that shapes us. We only get to choose what we do with the shape.

The book that follows is the long way around to a studio in Nairobi. It is also a love letter to the kerosene lamp and the chickens and the rain. None of those things are gone. They are still in the voice that introduces itself on Tuesday at 7:30. I never want them gone.
